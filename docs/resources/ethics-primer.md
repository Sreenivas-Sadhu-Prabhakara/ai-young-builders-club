# AI ethics for kids

*Background for facilitators + a kid-friendly version for Week 4. The goal isn't to scare
kids off AI — it's to make them the kind of person who uses powerful tools wisely.*

## The big idea

AI is a **power tool**. A power drill can build a treehouse or hurt someone — the tool isn't
good or bad, but *how people use it* really matters. AI is the same, just bigger. Learning to
use it wisely is part of being a builder.

We focus on four ideas kids can actually understand and act on: **truth, fairness, fakes,
and privacy.**

---

## 1. Truth — "AI can be confidently wrong"

AI predicts words that *sound* right. Sometimes those words are true; sometimes they're
made up. This is called a **hallucination**, and the scary part is the AI sounds just as
confident when it's wrong.

**Kid takeaway:** Treat AI like a clever friend who sometimes guesses. For anything that
matters (homework facts, health, news), **check it** with a real source or a trusted adult.

**Try it (Week 4):** Ask the AI for a "fact" about something you know well, or about a made-up
thing ("Tell me about the Great Sock War of 1850"). Watch it confidently invent stuff.

---

## 2. Fairness — "AI can be biased"

AI learns from examples humans collected. If those examples are lopsided, the AI becomes
lopsided too. If it mostly saw pictures of one kind of person doing a job, it might assume
*everyone* who does that job looks that way. That's **bias** — and it can be unfair to real
people.

**Kid takeaway:** AI isn't magically fair. It reflects the examples it learned from, warts
and all. Notice when it seems to assume things.

**Try it (Week 4):** Ask an image AI for "a picture of a [doctor / hero / scientist]" a few
times. Do they all look the same? Why might that be?

---

## 3. Fakes — "Seeing isn't believing anymore"

AI can make **deepfakes**: fake photos, videos, and voices that look and sound real. This is
amazing for movies and silly fun — and dangerous when someone uses it to trick people, bully,
or spread lies.

**Kid takeaway:** A photo or video being realistic doesn't mean it's real. Be a little
skeptical, especially if something is shocking. Ask: *where did this come from?*

**And:** never make a fake of a **real person** to fool or hurt them. That's a line we don't
cross. What you make says something about you.

---

## 4. Privacy — "What you type can be kept"

Many AI tools **store** what you type to improve themselves. So if you put in secrets — your
full name, address, passwords, private photos — those could be kept on someone else's
computer.

**Kid takeaway:** Treat an AI chat like a postcard, not a diary. **Never** put in personal
secrets. (This is club rule #1, and now you know *why*.)

---

## Being a good AI citizen — the kid pledge

A short pledge you can read together in Week 4:

> 1. I **check** important things AI tells me — I know it can be confidently wrong.
> 2. I remember AI can be **unfair**, because it learned from human examples.
> 3. I don't make **fakes** of real people to trick or hurt them.
> 4. I keep my **secrets** out of AI tools.
> 5. I use AI to **build and help**, not to cheat or be mean.

## Tougher questions kids might raise (and honest answers)

**"Is it cheating to use AI for homework?"**
It depends on what your teacher asked for and whether you actually *learn*. Using AI to
explain something so you understand it = great. Having it do the work so you skip learning =
that's cheating yourself. When unsure, ask your teacher.

**"Will AI take everyone's jobs?"**
It changes jobs more than it erases them — some disappear, many change, new ones appear. The
people who do best are the ones who learn to *work with* AI. (Like you're doing now.)

**"Could AI become evil like in movies?"**
Today's AI doesn't want anything — it has no feelings or goals; it predicts patterns. The
real risks right now are about **how people use it** (fakes, bias, scams), not robots taking
over. That's why *people* learning to use it well matters so much.

**"Is it bad for the planet?"**
Training and running big AI uses a lot of electricity and water for cooling. It's a real
trade-off people are working on. Using AI thoughtfully (not spamming it for no reason) is part
of being responsible.

!!! note "For facilitators"
    Keep it concrete and non-preachy. Anchor each idea to the **try-it** activity so kids
    *experience* the lesson rather than just hear it. The aim is thoughtful confidence, not
    fear.
